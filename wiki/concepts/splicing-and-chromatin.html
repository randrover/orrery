Splicing and Chromatin

Chromatin state is coupled to splicing through histone marks, adapter proteins, nucleosome positioning and RNA polymerase II elongation speed, which together influence cotranscriptional-splicing outcomes and the retention of pre-mRNA on chromatin lee-2015-mechanisms-and-regulation-of-alternative.

Histone marks, adapters, and nucleosomes

Histone modifications recruit splicing machinery and tune elongation. “Adapter” proteins physically bridge chromatin to the spliceosome and splicing-factors: CHD1 links chromatin to the U2 snRNP, and MRG15 (an H3K36me3 reader) recruits the repressor PTB to regulate alternative-splicing lee-2015-mechanisms-and-regulation-of-alternative. Marks such as H3K9ac and H3K9me2 modulate Pol II elongation and thus splicing outcomes marasco-2022-the-physiology-of-alternative. A documented feedback loop: G9a (EHMT2) exon-10 inclusion creates an H3K9me2-driven positive loop reinforcing neuronal differentiation marasco-2022-the-physiology-of-alternative.

Pol II speed and chromatin retention

Pol II elongation (~3–4 kb/min) sets the kinetic “window” for splice-site recognition; nucleosomes act as elongation barriers, and slow-polymerase variants shift splicing patterns lee-2015-mechanisms-and-regulation-of-alternative marasco-2022-the-physiology-of-alternative. Beyond synthesis, polyadenylated pre-mRNA can remain chromatin-associated long after transcription ends, where splicing completion gates release — making chromatin retention central to post-transcriptional-splicing choquet-2025-the-regulation-and-function-of.

Nuclear compartmentalization

Subnuclear localization correlates with splicing timing: ~31% of nuclear-speckle-associated retained introns overlap annotated detained introns, versus 12% for lamina-associated ones — implicating nuclear speckles and other compartments in coupling transcript position to splicing completion choquet-2025-the-regulation-and-function-of. How speckles mechanistically link localization to timing remains an open question choquet-2025-the-regulation-and-function-of.
