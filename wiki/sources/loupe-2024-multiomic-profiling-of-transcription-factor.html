Multiomic profiling of transcription factor binding and function in human brain

One-line: A large multiomic ChIP–seq resource (braintf) mapping occupancy of >100 transcription factors directly in human postmortem brain tissue and FANS-sorted neuronal/glial nuclei, integrated with ATAC-seq, RNA-seq and DNA methylation to model TF function and to link specific TFs (notably TBR1, PKNOX1) to neuropsychiatric disease risk.

Key takeaways

  • First and largest direct measurement of TF binding in human postmortem brain: ChIP–seq for 106 total TFs (96 in bulk, 76 in sorted nuclei) plus histone marks across nine brain regions from four neurotypical donors, paired with ATAC-seq, RNA-seq, phased WGS and DNA methylation — packaged as the braintf resource.
  • FANS sorting (NeuN+ neurons, Olig2+ oligodendrocytes, NeuN−/Olig2− microglia+astrocytes) de-confounds cell-type signal versus bulk homogenate; for nearly all TFs profiled, no prior brain binding data existed in ENCODE.
  • Identifies high-occupancy target (HOT) sites — loci bound by many TFs — that are promoter/CpG-island biased and largely cell-type-shared; they carry little TF-specific information and bias both motif discovery and computational TF-binding predictors. Removing them sharpens motif recognition for low-GC factors.
  • Neuronal TFs SATB2 and TBR1 bind unique, HOT-depleted cis-regulatory-elements and promote neuronal gene expression; 31% of TF-bound regions have no ENCODE cCRE annotation and are enriched for neuron-specific regulatory elements/pathways.
  • Stratified LD score regression links TF binding sites — especially in NeuN+ neurons (e.g. PKNOX1, TBR1, BCL11A) — to heritability for neuropsychiatric disorders (schizophrenia, bipolar), more strongly than histone marks or ATAC-seq; Alzheimer’s risk localizes to NeuN−/Olig2− (e.g. REST).

Notable claims / data

  • Union of TF peaks across the four large regions: 239,361 distinct regions spanning 6.94% of hg38 (text also cites 236,311 in Fig. 1); ~49% distal enhancer-like (dELS), 8% promoter-like (PLS), 31% unannotated (“none”). Promoters shared across more cell types than enhancers (P < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶).
  • “None” regions enriched for neuron-specific elements: odds ratio 2.9 for overlap with NeuN+ vs bulk ATAC-seq (P < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶) vs 1.1 (Olig2+) and 1.5 (NeuN−/Olig2−).
  • TF co-association: PCA on ChIP signal (first 20 PCs = 83.76% variance) yields three clusters — Cluster 1 low promoter-preference/high signal, Cluster 3 promoter-biased (H3K4me3-overlapping), Cluster 2 intermediate (TBR1–SATB2 node). Recovers known interactions (CTCF–RAD21 cohesin, EHMT2–REST, NFIB–NFIC).
  • HOT sites = top 10% of merged loci by number of bound TFs; 66% are promoter-like CpG-island sequences (OR = 3.81, P < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶); shared HOT sites enriched for housekeeping-gene promoters (OR = 8.245). SATB2 binds few-TF regions; HCFC1 binds predominantly HOT sites.
  • Motifs: from JASPAR 2022, peak motif-match proportion ranged 0.8–86.5%; of 49 factors, 24 were most enriched for their expected motif. Common motifs (e.g. THAP11, NRF/SP families) are GC-rich; removing HOT sites improved enrichment for low-GC motifs (e.g. NFIB motif rose to top-ranked MEME call).
  • Predictive models (Virtual ChIP–seq, 16 overlapping TFs): bulk-DLPFC auPRs correlated with GM12878 cell-line auPRs (r = 0.69, P = 0.0093); CTCF/NRF1 well predicted, EGR1/REST poorly. auPRs drop sharply when HOT sites removed — models are biased toward easily-predicted promoter/HOT sites and miss neuron-enriched “none” CREs.
  • Gene-expression modeling (NeuN+ linear models controlling for cobound TF count): most TFs positively affect expression, with largest effect at non-HOT promoters; at HOT promoters individual TFs have minimal effect. TAF1 most enriched for upregulated genes; EHMT2/SIRT1 repressive; TBR1/ZBTB7B dual activator/repressor.
  • Cell-type-biased TFs: SATB2/TBR1/BCL11A (neurons), OLIG2/SOX8 (oligodendrocytes), MITF (microglia/astrocytes). Example: a NeuN+ PKNOX1 peak overlaps a schizophrenia risk locus at the 3′ end of TSNARE1, linked to neuronal ADGRB1 and ARC expression.
  • Caveats: only four donors; ChIP–seq quality/skewness covaries with peak count and signal-to-noise; HOT sites should NOT be universally excluded (unlike ENCODE excluded-regions list) since they capture real GC-rich TF behavior; target-gene/distal links rely on a prior single-cell multiomic study (Anderson et al. 2023).

Connections

  • A functional-single-cell-genomics / regulatory resource: complements zhu-2026-integrative-analyses-elucidate-transcriptional-regulatory and groover-2026-mapping-cis-regulatory-mutations-at — all interpret noncoding cis-regulatory-elements, but this one supplies direct TF occupancy maps (ChIP–seq) rather than MPRA-measured activity.
  • Sibling human-brain regulatory/psychiatric-genetics source to li-2018-integrative-functional-genomic-analysis (PsychENCODE) and zhu-2026-integrative-analyses-elucidate-transcriptional-regulatory; uses cell-type FANS sorting like the sorted-nuclei strategy in kim-2020-extraction-of-distinct-neuronal-cell.
  • Conceptually adjacent to TF/RBP binding-map efforts such as encode-rbp-map (van Nostrand RBPs) — both build occupancy maps to assign regulatory function, but BrainTF targets DNA-binding TFs in brain tissue rather than RNA-binding proteins.
  • Relevant to the developing/diseased-brain hub brain-development-neurogenomics via neuronal TFs (SATB2, TBR1, BCL11A, NEUROD1) with established roles in cortical neuron development.

Open questions

  • Can the BrainTF occupancy maps improve in silico TF-binding predictors for the neuron-enriched, unannotated (“none”) CRE category that current models miss?
  • Do TF–disease enrichments replicate with more donors and additional disease GWAS, and can dual-role TFs (TBR1, ZBTB7B) be resolved into activator vs repressor contexts?
  • How do these bulk/sorted-nuclei TF maps relate to single-cell-resolution regulatory states, and to allele-specific binding from the phased WGS generated here?
