Integrative functional genomic analysis of human brain development and neuropsychiatric risks

One-line: The PsychENCODE/BrainSpan developmental flagship — a multimodal (mRNA-seq, smRNA-seq, scRNA-seq/snRNA-seq, DNA methylation, histone/CTCF ChIP-seq, genotype) atlas of 60 postmortem human brains spanning 5 postconceptional weeks to 64 years, revealing a cup-shaped transcriptomic trajectory anchored on a “late fetal transition” and showing how neuropsychiatric GWAS risk converges on specific developmental coexpression modules and cell types; alternative splicing is one of the molecular layers tracked across development.

Key takeaways

  • Generated and integrated genomics across 16 brain regions (11 neocortical areas + HIP, AMY, STR, MD thalamus, CBC) and the full prenatal-to-adult span: 1230 bulk samples from 48 brains plus 18,288 single cells/nuclei from 12 brains, across 60 de-identified, histopathologically unremarkable donors of both sexes and multiple ancestries.
  • A global “cup-shaped” (hourglass-like) transcriptomic pattern: high inter-/intra-regional variation in embryonic–mid-fetal ages, a sharp late fetal transition (window W5, ~35 PCW–0.3 PY) of collapsing regional differences, then renewed divergence in childhood–adolescence. Most protein-coding genes are temporally (67.8%) or spatially (54.5%) differentially expressed.
  • Alternative splicing tracks this developmental program: global splicing (the exon inclusion ratio, percent spliced in / PSI; see percent-spliced-in) is higher prenatally than postnatally, and genes with the highest interregional expression variation in a given window also showed higher PSI — leading the authors to argue the late fetal transition “may be amplified by alternative splicing.”
  • 68 RNA-binding proteins selected for their roles in RNA splicing (cf. the rna-binding-proteins / splicing-factors layer) themselves show a late fetal transition in expression by hierarchical clustering, consistent with a developmentally regulated splicing-regulatory program.
  • Neuropsychiatric GWAS risk converges developmentally: integrating GWAS + Hi-C + H3K27ac and WGCNA coexpression modules, disease risk genes (MEF2C, SATB2, SOX5, TCF4, TSHZ3, ZNF184, NR4A2, etc.) concentrate in a small number of spatiotemporally dynamic modules — most prominently module ME37, enriched for SCZ, IQ, neuroticism, ASD and broader neurodevelopmental-disorder risk and specific to deep-layer neocortical excitatory neurons. This grounds the source in splicing-and-disease’s neuropsychiatric wing (risk → developmental context), even though the disease mechanism here is largely regulatory/coexpression rather than splice-variant-based.

Notable claims / data

  • Sampling: transcriptome from 607 high-quality tissue mRNA-seq samples (16 regions, 41 brains, 8 PCW–40 PY); smRNA-seq on 278 QC-passing samples (16 regions, 22 postnatal brains); scRNA-seq from 1195 cells (9 brains, 5–20 PCW); snRNA-seq from 17,093 nuclei of adult DFC/DLPFC (3 brains). Epigenome: HumanMethylation450 in 269 postnatal samples; ChIP-seq for H3K4me3, H3K27me3, H3K27ac, and CTCF in DFC and CBC subsets.
  • Splicing-specific evidence is descriptive, not QTL-based. This paper quantifies global PSI dynamics and splicing-RBP expression across development (figs. S18–S19, table S7); it does not itself run eQTL/sQTL/TWAS. The companion comprehensive PsychENCODE resource paper (Wang et al., Science 362, eaat8464, 2018) houses the eQTL/isoform-QTL/cis-QTL integration — only referenced indirectly here.
  • Late fetal transition is supported by MDS/AC-PCA on gene and isoform levels, intra-/inter-regional Pearson correlation, and cell-type deconvolution; W5 samples cluster with early-postnatal rather than late-mid-fetal samples. Cerebellum (CBC) is the most transcriptomically distinct region and, once excluded, the cup-shape sharpens.
  • Disease convergence: partitioned LD-score regression found SNP heritability for SCZ, IQ and neuroticism enriched specifically in DFC- (not CBC-) specific H3K27ac elements, but no enrichment for non-neural traits (height, HBA1C/diabetes). 7/73 modules captured early-onset disorder risk (ADHD, SCZ, MDD); 5 modules captured adult-onset (AD, PD), the latter enriched for glial signatures (ME3, ME7 with AD risk).
  • Caveat raised by the authors: little overlap between GWAS-risk modules and modules differentially expressed in SCZ/BD/ASD postmortem brains — implying a possible decoupling of primary genetic causes from secondary postmortem expression changes, and emphasizing the value of studying neurotypical development.
  • Cross-species: a companion macaque study (Zhu et al., Science 362, eaat8077) shows the same global pattern, framing the late fetal transition as a conserved catarrhine-primate feature.

Connections

  • This is the developmental/spatiotemporal anchor for the brain-splicing cluster: it provides the prenatal-vs-postnatal PSI dynamics that brain-sQTL atlases sit on top of. Directly complements zhang-2020-regional-variation-of-splicing-qtls (GTEx brain-region sQTLs) and park-2018-the-expanding-landscape-of-alternative (population splicing variation) — Li 2018 supplies the developmental axis those QTL studies lack.
  • The “developmentally regulated splicing program” framing connects to kalsotra-2011-functional-consequences-of-developmentally-regulated (developmental splicing switches) and baralle-2017-alternative-splicing-as-a-regulator (AS as a developmental regulator).
  • Its neuropsychiatric-risk integration links to splicing-and-disease; the regulatory/coexpression-module mechanism here is a contrast to mis-splicing-centric disease accounts such as scotti-2015-rna-mis-splicing-in-disease.
  • Introduces the psychencode-brainspan-developmental-resource entity (development.psychencode.org / brainspan.org), a reusable multimodal human-brain data resource.
  • Because the paper itself does not generate sQTL/TWAS but motivates them, it bridges to splicing-qtl conceptually rather than methodologically.

Open questions

  • The paper measures global PSI but not which exons switch or which trans-regulators drive them developmentally — what are the specific developmental splicing events, and do they overlap with the brain sQTLs reported in zhang-2020-regional-variation-of-splicing-qtls?
  • Does the prenatal-high PSI signal reflect genuine isoform regulation or shifting cell-type composition (progenitor- vs neuron-enriched isoform programs) that bulk tissue confounds?
  • Could splice-variant QTLs (rather than the regulatory-element/coexpression signals used here) explain additional GWAS heritability for SCZ/ASD in the ME37 deep-layer-excitatory-neuron context?
  • Given the decoupling of GWAS-risk modules from postmortem-DEX modules, are disease-relevant splicing changes primary (developmental) or secondary (reactive) — and at which developmental window do they act?
