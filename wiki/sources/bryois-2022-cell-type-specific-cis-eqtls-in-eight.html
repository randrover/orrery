Cell-type-specific cis-eQTLs in eight human brain cell types identify novel risk genes for psychiatric and neurological disorders

One-line: A single-nuclei cis-eQTL map across eight adult human brain cell types (192 donors) showing that nearly half of genetic effects on expression are cell-type-specific — effects that bulk-tissue eQTLs systematically dilute — and co-localizing these eQTLs with GWAS to nominate disease risk genes and the specific cell types in which they act.

Key takeaways

  • Single-nuclei RNA-seq + genotyping in 192 adult brains (prefrontal cortex, temporal cortex, deep white matter) yields cis-eQTLs for eight major brain cell types, identifying 7,607 eGenes (4,799 unique genes) at 5% FDR.
  • 46% (3,537/7,607) of cell-type-level eQTLs show cell-type-specific effects, strongest in microglia — direct evidence that aggregating cell types in bulk tissue masks a large fraction of genetic regulation (the bulk-vs-cell-type confounding theme; cf. walker-2019-genetic-control-of-expression-and, zhang-2020-regional-variation-of-splicing-qtls).
  • Cell-type-level eQTLs have larger effect sizes and affect more constrained genes than tissue-level eQTLs; a “tissue-like” pseudo-aggregated re-analysis of the same data recovered far fewer eQTLs (3,058 vs the cell-type total) with smaller effect sizes, isolating cell-type resolution (not cohort) as the driver.
  • Co-localization with GWAS for neuropsychiatric and neurodegenerative disorders shows that at most loci a single gene co-localizes in a single cell type, pinpointing both the risk gene and its cellular context (e.g. microglial signals for AD/MS, excitatory-neuron and microglial signals for PD).
  • Explicitly an expression-only map: the authors flag as a limitation that they “did not measure splicing, which might play an important role in disease, potentially missing a subset of co-localization signals” — making this a complementary counterpart to cell-type/regional splicing-qtl studies and motivating cell-type sQTL work for splicing-and-disease.

Notable claims / data

  • Cohort/data: single-nuclei RNA-seq + genotyping on 270 brain samples from 148 individuals, integrated with 102 published single-nuclei samples → 192 genotyped donors total. cis window = SNPs within 1 Mb of the TSS; covariates = study, disease status, genotype PCs, expression PCs.
  • 7,607 eGenes at 5% FDR (4,799 unique). Authors estimate the true fraction of testable genes with an eQTL is at least 10–48%, so discoveries are power-limited. eQTL counts track nuclei abundance: 2,734 in excitatory neurons vs only 16 in pericytes.
  • Replication in bulk cortical Metabrain: 77–100% of SNP–gene pairs replicate at 5% FDR (91–100% concordant direction); neuronal eQTLs replicate slightly better (82%, pi1=86%) than glial (79%, pi1=86%). Effect-size correlation Rb = 0.74–0.94 (neurons 0.85 > glia 0.78).
  • Effect sizes larger than bulk: mean absolute beta 0.44 (cell-type) vs 0.34 (bulk Metabrain). The within-dataset “tissue-like” aggregation gave 3,058 eQTLs with significantly lower effect sizes — argues bulk RNA-seq underestimates genetic effects.
  • Positional enrichment: eQTLs enriched near the TSS; 3,027 in gene body, 2,907 upstream, 1,673 downstream; 47–56% of top eQTL SNPs hit the closest gene.
  • Co-localization: at 61–90% of co-localized loci a single gene co-localized, and usually in a single cell type. Disease examples — AD: APH1B (excitatory + others), NDUFS2/SPPL2A (oligodendrocytes only), ZNF226 at the APOE locus; PD: BIN3/CAMLG/GPNMB (multiple cell types), TMEM163 (microglia only), with complex 17q21 (MAPT/KANSL1) signals attributable to a common inversion; MS: 45 loci, microglial signals at CLECL1, IQCB1, TYMP, VEGFB, etc.
  • MS context-specificity: of 45 brain co-localized loci, 34 also co-localize in immune cell types; only 11 are brain-exclusive — same haplotype can regulate different genes in brain vs immune contexts, a caution against assuming cell-type assignment from co-localization alone.
  • Stated limitations: small sample vs bulk eQTL studies (limited power); no splicing measured; few discoveries in rare cell types (16 in pericytes); GWAS reflect disease risk not progression.

Connections

  • An adult, expression-axis complement to walker-2019-genetic-control-of-expression-and (prenatal bulk eQTL + sQTL): Walker dissects developmental-stage and the splicing layer in bulk tissue, whereas Bryois dissects the cell-type layer in adult tissue but omits splicing — the two confounders (developmental stage, cell-type composition) that bulk QTL maps conflate.
  • Directly relevant to the bulk-vs-cell-type-confounding theme shared with zhang-2020-regional-variation-of-splicing-qtls: both show that the resolution of the assay (region/cell type) determines which regulatory effects and disease co-localizations are even detectable.
  • The explicit “did not measure splicing” gap motivates cell-type-resolved splicing-qtl mapping; the GWAS co-localization framework here is the same one used to connect splicing-and-disease (sQTL–GWAS co-localization) to risk genes.
  • Microglia-dominated co-localization for AD/MS positions cell-type eQTLs as a tool for resolving which cell type a non-coding risk variant acts in — a question equally open for splicing QTLs.

Open questions

  • Cell-type-resolved sQTLs: how much additional, splicing-specific disease co-localization is missed by an expression-only map, and does splicing show the same microglia-skewed cell-type specificity?
  • Can larger single-nuclei cohorts and targeted enrichment of rare cell types (pericytes, COPs) recover the estimated 10–48% of genes with undetected eQTLs?
  • For MS loci shared between brain and immune cell types, which context is causal — and can cell-type-specific splicing help disambiguate pleiotropy from genuine multi-context regulation?
