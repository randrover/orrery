Emerging role of G-quadruplexes in alternative splicing regulation

One-line: A mechanistic review arguing that RNA G-quadruplexes (rG4s) are an emerging class of secondary-structure regulators of alternative-splicing, acting position-dependently near splice-sites to recruit or block splicing factors, with strong links to cancer and neurodegeneration and a therapeutic angle via G4 ligands.

Key takeaways

  • g-quadruplex structures are noncanonical four-stranded folds in guanine-rich nucleic acid: guanines pair via Hoogsteen (not Watson–Crick) bonds into stacked G-quartets, stabilised by K+ cations. They form in both DNA (dG4s) and RNA (rG4s) and act as rna-structure-and-splicing elements distinct from sequence motifs.
  • rG4s regulate alternative-splicing in a position-dependent way: located near donor (5’ ss) or acceptor (3’ ss) sites, they either recruit rG4-binding splicing-factors that redirect the spliceosome to proximal sites, or remodel pre-mRNA to mask/expose splice sites — functioning as enhancers or silencers (splicing-regulatory-elements).
  • This is a structure-based complement to the splicing-code: rG4s overlay sequence-defined splice-sites and splicing-regulatory-elements, and their effect depends on dynamic folding equilibria rather than a fixed consensus.
  • Many examples span all five major ASE types (exon skipping, intron retention, alternative 5’/3’ ss, mutually exclusive exons); rna-binding-proteins including the hnRNP family, FMRP, AFF family, SRSF1, and RBM25 read these rG4s.
  • dG4s also influence splicing indirectly via cotranscriptional-splicing: by slowing RNA Pol II elongation (and via R-loops on the non-template strand), they alter exon inclusion (Box 3), separate from the direct rG4 effects.
  • rG4-controlled splicing is heavily implicated in splicing-and-disease, especially cancer (EWS-FLI, hTERT, TP53, BCL-x, BCL-2, CD133, IGFN1) and neurodegeneration (BACE1/Alzheimer, FMR1/Fragile X).
  • G4 ligands (e.g. PhenDC3, pyridostatin, GQC-05) are model splicing-therapeutics that stabilise/unfold rG4s or block G4-binding proteins; effects across ASEs are often contradictory, so no general rule yet exists.

Notable claims / data

  • Scale: ~100,000 proteins from ~20,000 human genes via AS. ~60% of human transcripts contain ≥1 putative quadruplex-forming sequence (PQS); ~1,200,000 PQSs (300,000 unique motifs) across transcripts and splice junctions; canonical folding rule d(GGG+N1–7)x4 predicts ~400,000 dG4s in the genome.
  • A threefold enrichment of G4s near splice junctions, preferentially on the non-template (coding) strand (implying the corresponding rG4 matters); G4-at-ss enrichment is conserved in vertebrates but not invertebrates (possibly because AS matters less there).
  • FMR1 (Fragile X): two rG4s in exon 15 inhibit inclusion; FMRP binds them via its RGG motif and modulates its own splicing, favouring minor ISO2/ISO3 isoforms (autoregulatory feedback). ~15 loop mutations (ΔG4) altered exon-15 splicing without changing translation.
  • AFF family (AFF1–4; AFF2/FMR2 → FRAXE intellectual disability) bind rG4s via a conserved C-terminal domain (not RGG), localise to nuclear speckles, and broadly modulate AS.
  • CD44: intron-8 rG4 (element I-8) promotes the epithelial CD44v isoform; hnRNPF regulates it (depletion accelerates EMT). Alkaloids emetine and cephaeline modulate inclusion by unfolding rG4s (~60% of emetine-affected ASEs are skipped exons).
  • EWS-FLI (Ewing sarcoma): exon-8 rG4 promotes exon exclusion (keeping the oncoprotein); hnRNPH1 destabilises the rG4. Pyridostatin (PDS) blocks hnRNPH1 binding and reduces viability of EWSR1-fusion cells.
  • BCL-2: exon-3 rG4 favours skipping → proapoptotic BCL-2β; interplay with three nearby m6A sites; SRSF1 (an rG4-binding factor) resolves the rG4. Bcl-2β sensitises breast cancer cells to chemotherapy.
  • hTERT: intron-6 rG4s act as intronic splicing silencers; ligands Bisquinolinium 12459, CX-5461, and PhenDC3 shift splicing toward the inactive β transcript (telomestatin/BRACO19 had no effect — ligand selectivity).
  • TP53: intron-3 rG4 promotes intron-2 retention → dominant-negative ΔNp53/Δ40p53; ligand 360A shifts toward full-length p53. A common intronic polymorphism disrupting this rG4 is linked to increased cancer risk.
  • BCL-x (“rG4 saga”, Figure 1): exon-2 GQ-2/Q2 rG4 (overlaps the Gt16 G-tract) downstream of the alternative 5’ ss controls Bcl-xL (antiapoptotic) vs Bcl-xS (proapoptotic) balance. Splicing factor RBM25 binds Q2 directly via its arginine–glutamate (RE) domain — a novel G4-binding domain — and this binding is required for Bcl-xS production. Ligands GQC-05, PhenDC3, and the novel PhenDH8/PhenDH9 promote Bcl-xS; a genome screen for the Gt16-like G-tract found 3374 exons in 2707 genes.
  • Other cases: PAX9 intron-1 rG4 (positively regulates splicing; conserved in placental mammals; affects dentition — splicing-and-evolution); IGFN1 intron-15 (renal/breast cancer, PDS); CD133 introns 3/7 (cancer stem cells, RHPS4); BACE1 exon-3 alternative 5’ ss (hnRNPH/hnRNPL; Alzheimer); TRα/THRA alternative 3’ ss (G30 element, only suggestive evidence); vertebrate mirtron 5’-arm rG4s aiding mirtron biogenesis.
  • Mechanism (Box 6, splicing-and-chromatin/condensate angle): rG4–protein interactions may nucleate liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS) at nuclear speckles, concentrating splicing factors — proposed but not yet demonstrated for the validated rG4 cases.

Connections

  • Structure vs sequence: extends splice-sites and splicing-regulatory-elements from a sequence-motif view to a secondary-structure view — rG4s are folded ESE/ISE/ESS/ISS-like elements whose activity is position-dependent and dynamic, complementing the splicing-code.
  • Sits in the same RNA-structure thread as Lee 2015 (lee-2015-mechanisms-and-regulation-of-alternative), which discusses RNA secondary structure masking splice sites; this review supplies the G4-specific molecular detail and the new [[rna-structure-and-splicing]] entity.
  • splicing-and-disease: a curated cancer-centric catalogue (EWS-FLI, hTERT, TP53, BCL-x/BCL-2, CD133, IGFN1) plus neurodegeneration (BACE1, FMR1).
  • splicing-therapeutics: positions G4 ligands as splicing modulators; relates to BH3-mimetic (venetoclax) and SF3B1-modulator strategies, and to G4-inducing ASOs (G-ASOs) for exon skipping in Duchenne muscular dystrophy.
  • cotranscriptional-splicing: dG4/R-loop effects on Pol II elongation (Box 3) tie G4 biology to the transcription–splicing coupling thread.

Open questions

  • The molecular mechanisms of rG4-mediated AS, and the role of phase separation, remain largely unproven (mostly hypothesised models).
  • Which methods can confirm that an in-silico PQS actually forms a functional rG4 in cellulo and controls a specific gene’s AS (single-RNA-molecule monitoring is lacking).
  • G4 ligand selectivity is a major limitation: hundreds of thousands of competing dG4s/rG4s exist, effects across ASEs are often antinomic, and no general predictive “G4–ligand code” yet exists.
  • Is rG4-mediated splicing a general feature of apoptosis-regulating genes with opposing isoforms (e.g. BCL-x, MCL-1)?
  • Systematic identification of the full set of rG4-binding RBPs that reshape the AS landscape, and the influence of surrounding chemical RNA modifications (e.g. m6A), are still needed.
