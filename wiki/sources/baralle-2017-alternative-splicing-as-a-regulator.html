Alternative splicing as a regulator of development and tissue identity

One-line: A foundational review arguing that coordinated alternative splicing networks — driven mainly by developmentally regulated RNA-binding proteins and coupled to transcription/epigenetics — govern cell differentiation, tissue-identity acquisition and organ development across brain, heart, skeletal muscle, liver and other tissues.

Key takeaways

  • The field has shifted from cataloguing single splicing events to describing global, molecularly coordinated alternative-splicing networks; the central open challenge is demonstrating their functional/physiological consequences, not just their regulation.
  • splicing-factors / rna-binding-proteins (RBPs) are the principal coordinators of developmental splicing transitions: their expression levels, localization, activity and own alternative splicing are finely tuned per tissue and developmental stage. A tissue-specific “splicing-code” is proposed but hard to decipher because the same motif is read differently by different RBPs depending on context and binding position.
  • A recurring theme: genes regulated by developmental alternative splicing are often not changed in total expression level — splicing is the dominant regulatory layer for them (e.g. 31% of differentially spliced cortical genes show no expression change).
  • Splicing is frequently co-transcriptional, so cotranscriptional-splicing, Pol II elongation kinetics, chromatin modifications, nucleosome occupancy and DNA methylation modulate outcomes (splicing-and-chromatin); slow Pol II favors weak-splice-site recognition and exon inclusion. Coupling can run both ways (splicing of chromatin modifiers like EHMT2/LSD1 feeds back on the epigenome).
  • A major mechanistic motif is productive coupling of alternative splicing to NMD (“AS-NMD”): poison exons / intron retention introduce premature termination codons to downregulate proteins, prominently for RNA-processing regulators during cell differentiation (smooth muscle, erythropoiesis).
  • Misregulation of these same networks underlies disease — reversion of adult tissues to fetal/neonatal splicing patterns is a hallmark of heart failure and myotonic dystrophy (splicing-and-disease).

Notable claims / data

  • 90-95% of human genes undergo some alternative splicing; of ~20,000 protein-coding genes, high-resolution mass spectrometry finds ~37% generate multiple protein isoforms (caveat: not all AS events yield functional proteins; amplification methods can overestimate splicing events).
  • Brain: adult cortex vs embryos show ~400 differential AS events; 31% of developmentally AS-regulated genes do not change total expression. ptbp1/PTBP2/SRRM4 drive the neural progenitor→neuron transition (PTBP1 represses PTBP2 exon 10 → PTC+NMD; SRRM4 promotes its inclusion). RBFOX-depleted neurons misregulate >500 cassette exons. NOVA2 controls DAB1 exon 7b/7c skipping governing neuronal migration (rescued by short isoform in Nova2−/− mice).
  • Microexons (3-15 nt) regulated by SRRM4 tune protein-interaction domains in neurogenesis and are misregulated in autism (SRRM4 upregulation). Tau exon 10 sets 3R/4R isoform ratio; imbalance impairs APP axonal transport.
  • Heart: cardiomyocyte AS transitions concentrate in the first 4 weeks postnatally; sf3b1 is hypoxia-induced in cardiac hypertrophy, mis-splicing ketohexokinase exons toward a liver isoform that drives anabolic metabolism and contractile failure. CELF1/MBNL1 antagonistically regulate cardiac splicing.
  • Titin (TTN, highest exon count of any known gene): I-band exons 50-219 are developmentally regulated; neonatal long N2BA (3.7 MDa) → adult short N2B (2.97 MDa); ratio sets sarcomere passive tension and myocardium wall stiffness. RBM20 regulates this via a two-step splicing-repressor mechanism; RBM20 mutations cause human dilated cardiomyopathy. A CRISPR-Cas9 mouse blocking the Mex5/is7-containing TTN isoform produced a dystrophic phenotype in soleus and heart.
  • Myotonic dystrophy (most common adult muscular dystrophy): CTG/CCTG repeat expansions in DMPK 3′UTR (type 1) or CNBP intron 1 (type 2) sequester MBNL1 and activate CELF1, reverting adult tissues to fetal isoforms — e.g. INSR exon 11 (insulin resistance), CLCN1 exon 7a (myotonia), SCN5A (cardiac conduction defects); ~80% of patients develop cardiac dysfunction.
  • Other tissues: liver maturation requires ESRP2 (one of few postnatally induced RBPs) and SRSF3/SRSF10/SLU7; pancreas networks via NOVA1 and RBM4 (ISL1, PAX4 → insulin); spermatogenesis shows splicing-epigenetics coupling (MRG15 reads H3K36me3, recruits PTBP1/2 to splice TNP2; knockout → intron retention, round-spermatid arrest, sterility); T-cell activation feedback loop (CELF2↔JNK via MKK7 exon 2 skipping; HNRNPL/PSF control CD45). Erythropoiesis uses stage-specific exon 16 switching in protein 4.1R and MBNL1-regulated Ndel1 exon inclusion.
  • Caveat the authors stress repeatedly: dramatic splicing changes can have only slight physiological impact (e.g. fibronectin EDA transitions do not majorly affect mouse development), so genome-wide data must be paired with molecular validation.

Open questions

  • How to functionally characterize the thousands of developmentally regulated protein isoforms whose roles remain unknown (the authors call this the field’s biggest bottleneck)?
  • When does a large splicing change actually matter physiologically vs being tolerated (the FN1-EDA paradox)? What predicts functional impact?
  • Can a tissue-specific splicing code be learned given that cellular composition, RBP combinations and binding-position effects confound prediction from pre-mRNA sequence alone?
  • How general is splicing↔epigenetics bidirectional coupling in development beyond the few demonstrated loci (EHMT2, LSD1, MRG15/TNP2, CTCF/CD45)?
