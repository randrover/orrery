Functional consequences of developmentally regulated alternative splicing

One-line: A 2011 Nature Reviews Genetics review surveying how regulated alternative splicing networks reshape transcriptomes during development, differentiation, and cell stress across yeast, worms, flies, and vertebrates, and what physiological consequences those splicing transitions actually have.

Key takeaways

  • Frames the central question of the field: of the ~19,000 alternatively spliced human genes, what fraction of mRNA isoforms have a real functional impact? Much diversity is low-abundance, non-conserved stochastic noise, but a growing set of transitions are physiologically determinative.
  • Distinguishes two roles for alternative-splicing: determinative (the splicing change itself drives the biological outcome) versus permissive (splicing provides mRNA/protein variability that a downstream mechanism then uses).
  • Splicing transitions accompanying physiological change are disproportionately conserved (>40% of heart/muscle transitions conserved between mammals and birds, vs <20% genome-wide human-mouse), arguing for functional importance.
  • Genome-wide studies repeatedly find two largely separate gene sets: one regulated mainly at the level of total mRNA expression, the other mainly at the level of splice-isoform choice without changing total output.
  • A splicing regulatory network is defined as the set of events directly regulated by one RNA-binding protein; ~50 mammalian auxiliary regulators are characterized, each governing hundreds of events but determinative for only a subset.
  • Reviews concrete networks across biology: yeast meiosis (Mer1/Nam8/Tgs1), cell cycle/apoptosis (SRSF1, BCL-X, MCL1), stem-cell pluripotency (OCT4, RBFOX2, PTB/nPTB), EMT (SRSF1, ESRP1/2, FGFR2), heart development (CELF/MBNL/SR switch), brain development (DSCAM1, ROBO3, NOVA, RBFOX1), and stress responses (heat shock, genotoxic stress).

Notable claims / data

  • Alternative splicing affects ~90% of human genes, ~60% of Drosophila genes, ~25% of C. elegans genes (RNA-seq estimates).
  • In Drosophila development (27 stages) >60% of alternative splicing undergoes developmental change; in C. elegans (17 stages) >30% does, often in coordinated co-regulated sets.
  • Budding yeast: only 5% of genes (290 of 6,000) have introns, but introns are enriched in highly expressed genes — >1/4 of the ~38,000 RNAs transcribed per hour are spliced. ~45 intron-containing genes are inefficiently spliced in vegetative growth; 13 of 20 intron-containing meiotic genes are efficiently spliced only during meiosis. At least three overlapping meiotic splicing programs (Mer1 activates 4 single-intron genes; Nam8- and Tgs1-dependent subnetworks).
  • Cell cycle: a genome-wide RNAi screen with BCL-X/MCL1 splicing reporters found knockdown of 52 genes induced pro-apoptotic splicing of both; aurora kinase A loss degrades SRSF1; importantly the splicing response preceded mitotic arrest (parallel, not secondary).
  • ESCs: splice-isoform diversity is high in undifferentiated cells and decreases on differentiation. OCT4 yields three isoforms (OCT4A/B/B1) with distinct functions; RBFOX2 is essential for human ESC viability but not for differentiated/transformed cells. PTB represses neuronal nPTB via NMD-coupled exon-10 skipping; miR-124 silences PTB — first miRNA→splicing-network hierarchy.
  • EMT: SRSF1 upregulation produces pro-invasive ΔRON (RON exon-11 skipping); ESRP1/2 downregulation switches CD44v→CD44s (essential for EMT); FGFR2 IIIb/IIIc mutually exclusive choice is steered by ESRPs, RBFOX2, and PTB recruited via the H3K36me3 mark through adaptor MRG15 — linking the histone code to splicing.
  • Heart: postnatal development shows three temporally coordinated splicing-transition sets; >40% conserved mammal-to-bird. CELF1/CELF2 drop >10-fold postnatally while MBNL rises ~4-fold; forcing CELF gain + MBNL loss in adult hearts reverts >half the transitions to the embryonic pattern. miR-23a/miR-23b drive the >10-fold CELF1/CELF2 downregulation (Dicer1 deletion re-expresses fetal isoforms). SR-protein KOs (SRSF1, SRSF2 conditional → dilated cardiomyopathy by ~8 weeks; SRSF10 constitutive → mid-gestation lethality) each misregulate only a few unique transcripts.
  • Brain — permissive: Drosophila Dscam1 produces >19,000 extracellular variants; individual neurons express 14-50 stochastically; a single isoform suffices for homophilic repulsion but >1,000 isoforms are required population-wide for self/non-self discrimination. ROBO3’s two isoforms act as a binary midline-crossing switch (spatial restriction is post-transcriptional, not splicing-based).
  • Brain — determinative: NOVA regulates ~700 splicing events; uses a conserved YCAY positional activity code shared with Drosophila Pasilla; NOVA2 KO disrupts Dab1/reelin-pathway splicing causing neocortical migration defects. RBFOX1 (A2BP1) controls neuronal excitation; linked to epilepsy, intellectual disability, and autism.
  • Stress: heat shock inhibits splicing partly via rapid SRSF10 dephosphorylation (reversed within ~1 h) and partly via nuclear stress bodies (peak ~3 h, reversed over 10-12 h). Genotoxic stress: UV-induced MDM2/MDM4 exon skipping removes the p53-binding region (activating p53); two co-transcriptional mechanisms — reduced EWS-RNAPII coupling (more skipping) and slowed RNAPII elongation (more inclusion).
  • AS-NMD (alternative splicing coupled to nonsense-mediated decay): general mammalian rule — a stop codon >50 nt upstream of the last intron triggers NMD. SR and hnRNP gene families autoregulate via ultraconserved-element AS-NMD switches with “polar” feedback (SR activators: activation→NMD; hnRNP repressors: repression→NMD).
  • Caveat (Box 3): many RBP binding sites may be non-functional “sinks” that sequester the regulator rather than directing splicing — paralleling the miRNA-target paradox. Cell-heterogeneity caveat: <20% of heart cells are cardiomyocytes, so whole-tissue splicing transitions can be ambiguous; single-cell transcriptomics is proposed as the fix.

Open questions

  • Which binding sites of an RBP are genuine “physiological targets” versus inert molecular “sinks,” and what criteria (conserved sites, robust loss/gain-of-function response, regulation during conserved physiological change) reliably distinguish them?
  • For each network, is a natural change in RBP activity actually used to drive the transition in vivo, versus the regulator merely being permissive/required?
  • How are multiple stress mechanisms with different kinetics (e.g., SRSF10 dephosphorylation vs nuclear stress bodies) integrated during recovery?
  • Is alternative splicing “reset” during somatic-cell reprogramming to pluripotency, as transcription is?
  • How will single-cell transcriptomics resolve which cell type within a heterogeneous tissue actually undergoes a measured splicing transition?
