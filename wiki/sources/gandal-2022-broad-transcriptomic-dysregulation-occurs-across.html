Broad transcriptomic dysregulation occurs across the cerebral cortex in ASD

One-line: Pan-cortical bulk RNA-seq (725 samples, 11 cortical areas) plus snRNA-seq and methylation profiling showing that autism’s molecular pathology is widespread across the cortex — not confined to frontotemporal association areas — follows an anterior-to-posterior gradient peaking in primary visual cortex, attenuates normal regional identity, and reflects cell-type-specific (mainly excitatory-neuron and glial) gene and isoform dysregulation onto which rare and common ASD risk converge.

Key takeaways

  • Bulk rna-seq of 725 brain samples spanning 11 cortical areas from 112 post-mortem donors (49 idiopathic ASD, 54 neurotypical controls, plus dup15q cases) — a >3-fold increase in samples and regions over prior frontotemporal studies. Extends the developing/diseased-brain transcriptomics program of the lab (brain-development-neurogenomics).
  • ASD shows widespread transcriptomic change across the entire cortex with an anterior-to-posterior gradient: the greatest differences are in primary visual cortex (BA17), coincident with attenuation of the normal transcriptomic differences between cortical regions (attenuation of regional identity, “ARI”).
  • Splitting differential expression by feature type reveals distinct transcript- (isoform-) level signals beyond gene-level signals, with transcript changes of greater effect-size magnitude — supporting a substantial role for alternative-splicing and isoform expression in ASD (splicing-and-disease). Co-expression networks built on isoform quantifications “reflect both alternative splicing and alternative promoter usage.”
  • Single-nucleus RNA-seq (>250,000 nuclei, 6 ASD / 6 control, frontal/parietal/occipital) and methylation cell-type deconvolution show the bulk signature is driven mainly by within-cell-type gene-expression changes (especially excitatory neurons and glia), not by shifts in cell-type proportions.
  • Rare and common ASD genetic risk converge on a downregulated synaptic co-expression module (GeneM5), while common variation alone is enriched in an upregulated protein-chaperone/heat-shock isoform module (IsoformM37) — implicating protein homeostasis in ASD.
  • Directly extends and replicates parikshak-2016-genome-wide-changes-in-lncrna: GeneM5 overlaps the previously identified downregulated synaptic module CTX.M16, and dup15q again shows a concordant-but-larger-magnitude version of the idiopathic ASD signature.

Notable claims / data

  • 4,223 genes and 9,474 transcripts differentially expressed (DE) cortex-wide at FDR < 0.05 (linear mixed model).
  • Consistent ASD signature across all 11 regions with highly concordant effect sizes vs the whole-cortex signature; BA17 (primary visual cortex) had the most DE genes (3,264), 59% overlapping the whole-cortex set.
  • snRNA-seq: 26 cell clusters; cell-type-specific DE shows the same regional gradient — 3–4× more DE genes in occipital/parietal cell types than PFC (23,144 / 17,042 / 5,235 DE genes respectively across clusters at FDR < 0.001), the majority from excitatory neurons (occipital excitatory neurons strongest overall).
  • Cell-composition shifts are subtle: only nominal increases in astrocytes (AST1) and decreases in lower-layer excitatory neurons (EX7/EX8); none survived FDR. Methylation CTD (n = 36/group) gave high log2FC concordance with vs without proportion covariates (Spearman ρ > 0.7), so proportion changes do not explain the bulk signature.
  • 24 gene co-expression modules (9 down, 15 up in ASD) plus 61 isoform modules (39 non-overlapping with gene modules); 38 modules up/down in ≥1 region, 18 dysregulated cortex-wide. 90 of 96 gene+isoform modules enriched among cell-type-specific DE genes.
  • Cortex-wide modules: GeneM9 (up, neuronal, non-coding-enriched); GeneM32 (up, reactive astrocytes); GeneM24 (down, endothelial/pericyte, blood-brain-barrier). GeneM5 (down) carries synaptic-vesicle/cytoskeleton/plasticity genes, is enriched for ASD common variants AND rare de novo protein-disrupting mutations (e.g. GRIN2A, MYO5A, BTRC), and overlaps Parikshak et al. CTX.M16. IsoformM37 (up) is enriched for ASD common variants only and carries heat-shock/protein-folding genes (HSP family).
  • 13 regionally-variable modules all track normal anterior-posterior expression gradients; none enriched for ASD risk. BA17-specific examples: GeneM30 (OPC; hubs SOX4, SOX11) and GeneM4 (inhibitory-neuron; e.g. SCN9A).
  • Caveat: single post-mortem snapshot across ages 2-68 and both sexes cannot establish causality or distinguish primary from compensatory changes; snRNA-seq has few unique donors and poor transcript-level quantitation vs bulk; whether attenuated regional identity is a developmental arealization defect or a consequence of altered circuit/synaptic homeostasis remains open.

Connections

  • Direct successor to parikshak-2016-genome-wide-changes-in-lncrna (same lab) — scales the frontotemporal ASD cortex transcriptome to a pan-cortical map, reuses the regional-identity-attenuation and dup15q-concordance framework, and recovers the synaptic-downregulation module (CTX.M16 → GeneM5).
  • Splicing/isoform sibling for splicing-and-disease and alternative-splicing: transcript-level dysregulation exceeds gene-level signal and isoform modules capture splicing + promoter usage; consistent with splicing as an independent axis of ASD molecular pathology.
  • Core human psychiatric-genetics node for the brain-development-neurogenomics hub — links cortical arealization, cell-type-specific expression, and ASD common+rare risk convergence.
  • Uses bulk and single-nucleus rna-seq plus methylation-based deconvolution as complementary modalities.

Open questions

  • Is attenuation of regional identity (ARI) a primary early-developmental arealization defect or a downstream consequence of altered local circuit/synaptic homeostasis propagated across cortex?
  • Which transcript/splicing-level changes are causal vs compensatory, and how do ASD risk genes mechanistically reshape cortical patterning and connectivity?
  • Why is primary visual cortex (BA17) the epicenter — does it relate to the pervasive sensory-processing differences in ASD (now in DSM-5)?
  • How would pan-cortical, cell-type-specific genomic profiling during early human development, or regionally-patterned organoids, test the developmental-arealization hypothesis?
