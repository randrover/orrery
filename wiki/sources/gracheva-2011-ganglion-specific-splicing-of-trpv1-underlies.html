Ganglion-specific splicing of TRPV1 underlies infrared sensation in vampire bats

One-line: Shows that vampire bats detect infrared radiation not through a new receptor but through tissue-specific alternative splicing of TRPV1 — a trigeminal-ganglion-specific short isoform with a lowered heat-activation threshold — a striking example of how alternative-splicing tunes a channel’s function for evolutionary/physiological adaptation.

Key takeaways

  • Vampire bats (Desmodus rotundus) tune the already heat-sensitive channel TRPV1 by alternative-splicing to produce a truncated short isoform (TRPV1-S) with a heat-activation threshold lowered to ~30 °C, enabling infrared detection of warm-blooded prey.
  • The splicing event occurs specifically in trigeminal ganglia (TG), not in dorsal root ganglia (DRG) — “ganglion-specific splicing” — so TRPV1 keeps its normal noxious-heat role in body somatic afferents while acting as an infrared sensor in facial pit-organ afferents.
  • TRPV1-S lacks 62 C-terminal amino acids (a truncated cytoplasmic C-terminal domain), produced by inclusion of an alternative exon (exon 14a) carrying a stop codon; it constitutes 35–46% of TRPV1 transcripts in vampire bat TG but <3% in DRG.
  • This is a general mechanism across Laurasiatheria (cows, dogs, moles) whose Trpv1 gene shares the exon-14a-based splicing architecture — supporting a close phylogenetic relationship of bats with this superorder (splicing-and-evolution).
  • Demonstrates that evolutionary/physiological tuning of a sensory receptor can be achieved by regulated splicing rather than gene duplication or new channel co-option (contrasting with snakes, which use a non-heat-sensitive channel TRPA1 for infrared sensing).
  • The bat Trpv1 locus structure (canonical GT/AG splice sites flanking exon 14a; a strong tandem 5′ splice site CAGCAG; weak exon-15 acceptor) is highly conserved among microbats (>90% identity), and mini-gene assays show exon 14a is competent to splice but disfavored in the exon14–15 competition except in the TG environment.

Notable claims / data

  • Deep sequencing of poly(A)+ RNA from vampire-bat vs fruit-bat (Carollia brevicauda) TG and DRG identified the novel short TRPV1 isoform; TRPV1-S is 35–46% of TRPV1 transcripts in vampire TG, <3% in DRG, and barely detectable (<6%) in fruit-bat TG or DRG.
  • Functional characterization in HEK293 / Xenopus oocytes: TRPV1-S activates at a substantially lower threshold than TRPV1-L (30.5 ± 0.7 °C vs 39.6 ± 0.4 °C in HEK293; ~31 vs ~40 °C in oocytes) — a ~10 °C shift consistent with a role in infrared detection.
  • Co-expression of TRPV1-S and TRPV1-L yields an intermediate threshold (~33.9 ± 1.2 °C), suggesting short and long isoforms form functional heteromultimeric channels.
  • Vampire-bat TG (but not DRG) shows anatomical specialization toward large-diameter neurons, resembling pit-bearing snakes, indicating parallel adaptation of the TG for infrared sensation.
  • The 23-bp insertion in TRPV1-S cDNA maps to exon 14a; the 5.5-kb Trpv1 region contains a 23-bp exon 14a flanked by two introns (2.1 and 3.1 kb).
  • Across Laurasiatheria orders (Cetartiodactyla, Carnivora, Lipotyphla), exon 14a is present with distinct sequences/positions; in cows an in-frame insertion makes an extra-large TRPV1-XL, while microbats/megabats/moles have a stop-codon exon 14a like vampire bats.
  • Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of Trpv1 gene organization supports grouping bats with horses/dogs/cows/moles (Laurasiatheria) rather than with rodents.
  • Zebrafish TRPV1 (threshold ~32.9 °C) shows convergent thermosensory adaptation via a different C-terminal mechanism.

Connections

  • A canonical case study for alternative-splicing as a source of functional/proteomic diversity and for tissue-specific (here ganglion-specific) splicing regulation — complements the splicing-code framework (barash-2010-deciphering-the-splicing-code) with a concrete adaptive phenotype.
  • Strong example for splicing-and-evolution: regulated splicing (not new genes) drives sensory adaptation, and Trpv1 splice-site architecture serves as a phylogenetic marker supporting mammalian relationships.
  • Relates to broader themes of C-terminal isoform switching altering ion-channel/receptor properties; introduces the entities trpv1 and desmodus-rotundus to the wiki.
  • Illustrates how a cis-driven splice-site competition (exon 14a vs exon 15) is resolved differently by the trans environment of specific neuronal populations — echoing tissue-specific trans-factor logic seen for splicing-factors.

Open questions

  • Which trans-acting splicing-factors present in trigeminal-ganglion neurons (but not DRG) drive the ganglion-specific inclusion of exon 14a?
  • How does C-terminal truncation mechanistically lower the TRPV1 heat-activation threshold at the channel-structure level?
  • Do heteromeric TRPV1-S/TRPV1-L assemblies set a graded threshold tuned to prey body temperature in vivo?
  • How widely is receptor/ion-channel function across species tuned by analogous tissue-specific splicing rather than sequence changes?
