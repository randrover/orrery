U-rich elements drive pervasive cryptic splicing in 3′ UTR massively parallel reporter assays

One-line: Revisiting a 3′ UTR massively-parallel-reporter-assay (PTRE-seq), the authors discover that AU-rich elements (AREs) trigger pervasive cryptic splicing — mostly from an unannotated splice donor in the GFP coding sequence to acceptors in the 3′ UTR — which confounds MPRA measurements of post-transcriptional regulation.

Key takeaways

  • Repurposing the PTRE-seq MPRA (642 reporters combining let-7, Pumilio, Smaug, and ARE regulatory modules in a GFP 3′ UTR), the authors used “extended” primers spanning the full transcript and found widespread cryptic splicing invisible to standard short barcode-based readouts.
  • Splicing initiates from a weak, unannotated cryptic 5′ donor within the eGFP coding sequence (located at the stop codon) and proceeds to diverse weak 3′ acceptor sites in the reporter 3′ UTRs (splice-sites usage from non-canonical/weak sites).
  • U-rich sequences / AREs are the principal drivers: splicing depends almost entirely on the presence and position of ARE modules, which act as potent position-dependent regulators of both 5′ and 3′ splice-site choice and overall splicing efficiency (splicing-regulatory-elements).
  • AREs function context-dependently: an intronic ARE acts as a polypyrimidine tract activating a downstream 3′ acceptor (splicing enhancer), while an exonic/5′-adjacent ARE represses splice-site usage (splicing silencer) — a single ARE mechanism producing both enhancement and repression.
  • Cryptic splicing has diverse, confounding impacts on reporter expression — both increases (via “RE-excision” removing destabilizing 3′ UTR elements and enhanced production) and decreases — meaning it distorts conclusions drawn from 3′ UTR MPRAs about post-transcriptional regulation.
  • The cryptic splicing is constitutive and generalizable: reproducible across HeLa, HEK293, SH-SY5Y (neuroblastoma), and U87 (glioblastoma) cells (Pearson R ≥ 0.94), and validated by RT-PCR on individually transfected reporters.
  • Provides preventative strategies: synonymous recoding of the GFP cryptic donor (e.g. AAG→AAA at −2, CTG→CTA at −4) suppresses splicing without reducing GFP protein levels — a practical fix for MPRA designs.

Notable claims / data

  • Of 642 reporters, 119 (18%) were spliced at ≥20%, and 28 (5%) nearly completely (>90%); a cryptic 5′ site at the GFP stop codon featured in 165 splicing events (>1% usage).
  • RNA read categories (HeLa): 19% spliced, 30% full length, 14% palindromic indels (a separate cloning artifact, 78-bp palindromic insertion), 37% other anomalies; the “no spacer” library reproduced splicing (R=0.7) confirming it is intrinsic to the repetitive library, not the palindromic artifact.
  • Splice sites are weak: the GFP donor is 1st-percentile relative to annotated human donors yet supports up to 100% splicing; 3′ acceptors are 1st–28th percentile by MaxEntScan.
  • Position rules: a single 3′ UTR ARE module is sufficient to activate strong splicing; more intronic ARE modules → higher splicing at weaker acceptors; a 5′-adjacent (exonic) ARE blocks the downstream P-position donor; splicing occurs at the most proximal AG downstream of the 3′-most ARE; introns shorter than ~70 nt (minimum for spliceosome assembly) go unspliced.
  • Expression: observed splicing fraction strongly correlates with reporter expression (R=0.74); highly spliced reporters (>90%) show 2–3-fold enhanced production and >4× higher expression than baseline “blank” 3′ UTRs, explained by an “RE-excision” model where splicing removes destabilizing 3′ UTR elements.
  • MarathonRT/SuperScript II choice affected palindromic-artifact rates; splicing fractions robust across reverse-transcriptases and library preps.
  • Caveats: findings stem from one repetitive synthetic library (PTRE-seq); the exact quantitative cryptic-splicing rate depends on RT enzyme and library prep; broader implication (that other 3′ UTR MPRAs are affected) is argued but shown for this dataset in depth.

Connections

  • A cautionary, mechanism-rich study of cryptic splicing and weak splice-sites activation, directly relevant to splicing-methods and massively-parallel-reporter-assay design — cryptic splicing as a confounder of MPRA measurements.
  • Establishes U-rich/ARE sequences as potent, position-dependent splicing-regulatory-elements acting as both enhancers (polypyrimidine-tract-like) and silencers; connects to rna-binding-proteins (ARE-binding proteins) and percent-spliced-in-style quantification.
  • The theme of weak/cryptic splice-site activation by U-rich context complements genome-wide cryptic-splicing/mis-splicing findings (relates to garcia-ruiz-2025-splicing-accuracy-varies-across-human) and polypyrimidine-tract recognition mechanisms.
  • Practical relevance to reporter-based cis-regulatory-elements and 3′ UTR post-transcriptional-regulation studies generally.

Open questions

  • Which specific ARE-binding RBPs mediate the enhancer vs silencer behavior, and how do they interact with the spliceosome at weak sites?
  • How many published 3′ UTR MPRA datasets are confounded by undetected cryptic splicing, and how much do their conclusions change once corrected?
  • Can the position-dependent ARE splicing rules be turned into predictive design rules to engineer or avoid cryptic splicing in reporters and therapeutics?
  • Does ARE-driven cryptic splicing at weak sites occur in endogenous 3′ UTRs, and does it contribute to human disease mis-splicing?
