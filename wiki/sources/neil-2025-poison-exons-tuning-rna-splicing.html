Poison exons: tuning RNA splicing for targeted gene regulation

One-line: A review (from Remix Therapeutics) of poison exons (PEs) — alternatively spliced exons whose inclusion introduces a premature termination codon that routes the host mRNA to nonsense-mediated decay (NMD) — covering their role in endogenous gene regulation, their misregulation in disease (notably neurodevelopmental disorders and cancer), and how splice-modulating ASOs and small molecules exploit them to drug “undruggable” targets.

Key takeaways

  • Poison exons couple alternative-splicing to NMD (“AS-NMD”): inclusion of a PE inserts a premature termination codon (PTC), and because exon-junction complexes (EJCs) downstream of a stalled ribosome recruit UPF1/SMG1, the transcript is preferentially degraded. This makes PE inclusion a tunable knob on host-gene abundance, a form of post-transcriptional-splicing regulation rather than proteome diversification.
  • PEs are detected via short-read rna-seq (exon causing a PTC >50 nt upstream of an EJC), but many are efficiently degraded and only surface under NMD perturbation (UPF1/SMG5/6/7 knockdown or chemical NMD inhibition), EJC RNA-immunoprecipitation, capture sequencing of NMD intermediates, or long-read-rna-seq; computational/ML methods increasingly predict functional PEs.
  • PE misregulation drives disease in both cis (SRE/splice-sites mutations shifting PE inclusion) and trans (splicing-factors/rna-binding-proteins dysfunction). Neurodevelopmental disorders are especially affected because of long introns and embedded regulatory elements.
  • PEs are a therapeutic opportunity (splicing-therapeutics): ASOs can skip a pathogenic PE to restore expression (haploinsufficiency), or small molecules can induce PE inclusion to degrade a disease driver — analogous to PROTAC/molecular-glue targeted protein degradation but acting on RNA.
  • Two mechanistically distinct splice modulators of SMN2 are approved (nusinersen, risdiplam) and ~6 PE-targeting drugs are in clinical trials across oncology and non-oncology indications, signaling a maturing modality.

Notable claims / data

  • 90–95% of human genes produce multiple isoforms via alternative splicing; large-scale reporter assays imply >1 million autonomous exons in the human genome.
  • ~15% of all mRNAs are NMD targets; ~5% of exons (in ~13% of genes) show differential expression attributable to AS-NMD; many genes incorporate PEs into >20% of their expressed transcripts. PE enrichment is highest in introns with weak splice sites and in long introns.
  • PEs are over-represented in NMD-deficient RNA-seq datasets and show stronger predicted splice-site strength and greater cross-species conservation than “noise” cryptic events. A CRISPR screen estimated up to 50% of PEs may affect cellular fitness; computational models suggest possibly >1 million PEs in the human transcriptome.
  • Neuronal development: ~3000 alternatively spliced exons are coordinated by antagonistic RBPs and ~33% of such cassette exons are predicted to trigger NMD on inclusion or exclusion. Disease examples: a PE in FLNA (periventricular nodular heterotopia); TDP-43 mislocalization in ALS causing cryptic exon inclusion incl. a UNC13A PE that drives NMD-mediated loss of UNC13A.
  • Cancer: loss-of-function RBM10 (lung adenocarcinoma) and gain-of-function sf3b1 (myeloid cancers, altered 3’ splice-site selection / frameshifts) reshape the NMD-targeted transcript landscape; SR-protein PEs are autoregulated and dysregulated in tumors.
  • Therapeutics (Table 1): ASOs — STK-001/zorevunersen skips a SCN1A PE for Dravet syndrome (Phase 1/2a–2/3); STK-002 reduces an OPA1 PE for ADOA; smn2-targeting eteplirsen (DMD, approved) and nusinersen (SMA, approved). Small molecules (U1 snRNP–5’ss molecular glues) — risdiplam (approved, SMA), branaplam (HD; VIBRANT-HD halted for peripheral neuropathy), PTC518 (HD; ~43% mHTT reduction in CSF at 10 mg), SKY-0515 (HD/PMS1; ~72% HTT mRNA reduction in blood at 9 mg), REM-422 (MYB degrader, ACC/AML/MDS, Remix), RGT-61159 (MYB, Rgenta). Beyond PEs: BPN-15477/PTC258/PTC680 for ELP1 (familial dysautonomia), SKY-1214 inducing FANCL/FANCI exon skipping.
  • NMD escape: some PE-containing transcripts evade NMD and translate potentially harmful truncated peptides — PE inclusion is not always simple loss-of-function.

Open questions

  • Of the vast number of PEs, which trigger efficient degradation, and how can NMD penetrance be predicted up front to pick tractable targets?
  • Which splicing regulatory elements are most amenable to selective small-molecule/ASO modulation, and how can off-target effects of motif-targeting small molecules be predicted?
  • Can splice-switching small molecules rescue low gene expression (via PE skipping) as effectively as ASOs while keeping small-molecule delivery advantages?
  • Can paralog-selective targeting be achieved by exploiting faster-evolving intronic SREs between near-identical proteins?
