Splice-Junction-Based Mapping of Alternative Isoforms in the Human Proteome

One-line: An RNA-seq-guided proteomics method (and open-source tool, jcast) that translates alternative splice-junction pairs into compact tissue-specific protein databases to detect noncanonical and undocumented protein isoforms by mass spectrometry across 12 human tissues and during iPSC-cardiomyocyte differentiation.

Key takeaways

  • Bridges the transcript-to-protein gap in alternative splicing: most splicing isoforms detected by RNA-seq have never been confirmed at the protein level, and standard MS protein databases (SwissProt ~1.1 isoforms/gene) preclude their identification.
  • Builds junction-centric databases by extracting AS events with rMATS (rmats) — A3SS, A5SS, MXE, SE (skipped exon), RI (retained intron) — filtering on skipped-junction read counts (Gaussian mixture model cutoff, ~4 reads in heart) and replicate consistency (p ≤ 0.01), then enforcing one-frame in-silico translation (annotated frame or a frame avoiding premature termination codons) to avoid the false-positive inflation of 3-/6-frame translation.
  • Translated junction pairs are stitched back to full-length SwissProt canonical sequences via a 10-aa overhang so that junction-spanning peptides are searchable; orphan slices are discarded.
  • Reanalyzing ~80 million public mass spectra (5 ProteomeXchange datasets) across 12 human tissues recovered >1,500 noncanonical protein isoforms, including ~400 sequences undocumented on TrEMBL/RefSeq.
  • Demonstrates a functional consequence of splicing: alternative isoform regions preferentially overlap intrinsically disordered regions and post-translational-modification (PTM) sites, suggesting AS regulates protein function via disordered-region rewiring and PTM-site toggling.

Notable claims / data

  • From ENCODE RNA-seq of 12 GTEx tissues: median 72,194 AS events/tissue (adrenal fewest 66,160; testis most 91,895). AS-type distribution: SE 65.1%, A3SS 10.8%, RI 10.1%, MXE 7.3%, A5SS 6.7%.
  • Custom databases are far sparser than standard ones: heart database 13,816 entries vs SwissProt human 42,237 (20,225 canonical + 22,012 isoform), TrEMBL 93,555, RefSeq 113,620 — only 6.3%–17.0% the size of RefSeq. Mean across 12 tissues 11,911 entries (pancreas fewest 6,309; testis most 19,285).
  • Reanalyzed 1,927 MS files / 79.6 million MS2 spectra. In heart left ventricle, 13,900 distinct peptides at 1% FDR (reverse decoy + Percolator); 397 (2.9%) absent from SwissProt canonical, 142 not in SwissProt canonical+isoform, 39 not in TrEMBL.
  • Overall: 3,418 distinct uniquely-mapped peptides not in canonical SwissProt → up to 1,555 noncanonical isoforms in 1,189 genes. 1,385 peptides (681 genes) absent from SwissProt(canonical+isoform); 566 also absent from TrEMBL; 453 (366 genes) not in RefSeq. Testis and heart most enriched in noncanonical isoforms.
  • MXE has higher-than-expected translational rate (16.3% of peptides vs 7.2% of RNA events); RI lower (5.6% vs 10.0%); SE proportionate (~64.6% vs 64.8%). Titin (TTN) yielded the most isoforms.
  • Validation lines of evidence: 70.8% of undocumented peptides not matchable to RefSeq even with 2 mismatches (so not SAAVs); MSFragger open search (–200/+400 Da) on left ventricle found only 1 of 51 undocumented peptides confidently at 1% FDR, while 267/394 (67%) TrEMBL-noncanonical were matched — indicating added identification power. Targeted PRM with heavy synthetic standards verified PKM1/PKM2 and detected 6/12 undocumented endogenous junction peptides in heart lysate.
  • Example: a MYBPC3 SE isoform (peptide RTDSHEDTGILDFSSLLK) differs from canonical by only 10/1,274 residues but lies in a disordered region enriched for PKA phosphorylation sites (Fisher p=0.02; S275/S284/S304), with cardiac diastolic relevance. MYOM1 SE isoform region shows higher disorder (Mann-Whitney p=3.3e-50). Proteome-wide, alternative regions are significantly more disordered.
  • iPSC-cardiomyocyte differentiation (original 10-plex TMT shotgun proteomics, 3 iPSC lines, 14-day protocol): 87 quantified noncanonical isoforms (14 not in SwissProt), with stage-specific regulation (e.g., ACTN4, TPM1 isoforms incl. TPM1-kappa, NCAM1 N-CAM 140/180, PDLIM5). Transcript-vs-protein isoform logFC correlated (Pearson r 0.57–0.74 noncanonical; 0.52–0.57 canonical).
  • Caveats stated by authors: label-free single-junction-peptide quantification is imprecise; short-read RNA-seq produces combinatorially redundant/ambiguous junctions; bottom-up MS cannot resolve full-length isoforms; some undocumented matches with unexpected retention times are likely false positives; long-read RNA-seq and top-down MS would mitigate.

Connections

  • Method is built directly on rMATS (rmats) for AS event quantification and uses the percent-spliced-in (PSI) framework; complements PSI-based transcript tools by adding a protein-level readout.
  • A proteomics counterpart to other splicing-methods sources in this wiki (e.g., shen-2014-rmats-robust-and-flexible-detection); where most catalogued tools quantify splicing at the RNA level, this one asks which junctions are actually translated.
  • Supports the broader theme of alternative-splicing expanding proteome diversity and links splicing to protein structure/function via disordered regions and PTMs.
  • The named software deliverable is jcast.

Open questions

  • How many additional translatable junctions remain hidden due to short-read combinatorial ambiguity, and how much would long-read RNA-seq + top-down/middle-down proteomics recover?
  • Can elution-time (retention-time) prediction be improved enough to adjudicate borderline undocumented isoform peptides and lower false-positive risk?
  • Are MXE events genuinely more translatable, or is the apparent enrichment a reading-frame-conservation artifact?
  • Better NMD prediction from frameshifted/PTC-containing junctions to decide which transcripts truly yield protein.
