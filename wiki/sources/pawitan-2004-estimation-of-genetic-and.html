Estimation of genetic and environmental factors for binary traits using family data

One-line: A general GLMM/liability-threshold method, fitted by likelihood over arbitrary extended-family structures, for partitioning a binary trait into maternal, foetal, and environmental variance components — demonstrated on Swedish birth-registry pre-eclampsia data.

Key takeaways

  • Treats a binary disease phenotype as a thresholded unobserved normal “liability”, fitting it directly as a probit-link generalized-linear-mixed-model — a liability-threshold-model embedded in a variance-components-model. This handles arbitrary family structures (not just twins/relative-pairs) and incomplete outcome data.
  • Motivated by the limits of classic twin-and-family-study-design: twinning is rare so twin studies are underpowered for common conditions (PE prevalence ~4%), and nuclear families alone cannot separate maternal vs foetal genetic effects or genetic vs shared-environment effects. Extended family data (e.g. families of sib-pairs) breaks these confoundings.
  • Inference is by direct (exact) likelihood: the random effects are integrated out, reducing each family’s contribution to an orthant probability of a multivariate normal, evaluated via a fast Cholesky-based Monte Carlo algorithm (Genz). Parameters estimated by a derivative-free simplex; the authors note ongoing work on a hierarchical-likelihood approach to avoid the integration.
  • heritability is defined on the liability scale as h² = σ²_g/(σ²_g + σ²_e + 1), consistent with standard biometrical-genetics quantitative-genetics; with the probit link it is the proportion of liability variance due to additive genetic effects.
  • Families of sib-pairs (mothers who are full sisters, fathers unrelated, etc.) let the model separate maternal genetic (m), foetal genetic (f), common-family-environment (e), and common-sibling-environment (s) components, and can even split the foetal effect into maternal- vs paternal-transmitted contributions (f_m, f_p).

Notable claims / data

  • Twin study (Ros et al., ref [6]) of PE: 917 MZ + 1199 DZ Swedish female twin pairs, only 8 concordant MZ / 2 concordant DZ affected pairs → heritability estimate 0.57 but with 95% CI (0.0–0.71) — illustrating the power problem.
  • Familial clustering far exceeds chance: among women with 2 pregnancies, 570 had PE in both vs 68 expected under randomness; among 3-pregnancy women, 21 PE-in-all-three vs <1 expected. Clustering by fathers is similar (536 vs 63), showing nuclear data cannot separate maternal from paternal genetic contribution.
  • Application data: all Swedish pregnancies 1987–1997, first 3 known same-partner pregnancies; 239,193 family-pairs, 774,858 pregnancies, 20,358 pre-eclamptic. Mother–mother, father–father, and mother–father sib-pair groupings used.
  • PE risk is much higher in first (13,072/320,512 = 4.1%) than subsequent (7,286/454,346 = 1.6%) pregnancies; modelled via separate prevalence parameters φ₁, φ₂ orthogonal to the variance components.
  • Model 2 estimates (liability variance components): maternal σ²_m = 1.08 (0.99–1.18), foetal σ²_f = 0.63 (0.32–0.81), family-env σ²_e = 0.40 (0.33–0.51), sibling σ²_s = 0.02 (0.0–0.03). Maternal heritability h²_m = 1.08/(1.08+0.63+0.40+0.02+1) = 35%; foetal 20%, family-env 13%, sibling 1%.
  • Model 3 (split foetal effect) shows no evidence of unequal maternal vs paternal foetal contribution (Δ −2logL = 2.5, 1 df). Model 4 (later-pregnancy genetic/family interaction terms) is not a significant improvement (Δ = 0.1).
  • Conclusion: PE has both maternal and foetal genetic origin (part of the foetal effect from paternal genes), plus a real common-family environment effect but no detectable sibling-environment effect; goodness-of-fit (χ² = 656.3, 434 df for patterns with expected freq > 5) shows Model 2 explains most but not all variation.

Connections

  • Extends the same GLMM/liability logic used in rabe-hesketh-2008-biometrical-modeling-of-twin-and (which fits twin/family biometrical models with gllamm) from twins/relative-pairs to arbitrary extended pedigrees, and replaces approximate quadrature with exact orthant-probability likelihood.
  • Shares the variance-components-model foundation that mcardle-2005-mixed-effects-variance-components-models frames as the path-analysis ↔ VCM equivalence; here the same additive-genetic correlations (MZ=1, DZ/full-sibs=0.5, cousins=0.125) are encoded directly in random-effect correlation (R) matrices rather than path diagrams.
  • The probit/liability-threshold framing makes binary biometrical-genetics a special case of the generalized-linear-mixed-model, and the heritability definition aligns with classical quantitative-genetics (Falconer; Sham 1998).

Open questions

  • Can the hierarchical-likelihood (h-likelihood) formulation, flagged as ongoing work, deliver the same estimates without the Monte Carlo integration and known to fail for small clusters under PQL/APHL?
  • How to incorporate continuous fixed covariates without the data-grouping step that the current efficient likelihood computation requires?
  • Non-additive genetic effects (dominance, gene–gene, maternal×paternal interaction) are confounded with the family-environment effect here — what design or data resolves them?
  • Given that the estimated genetic components still leave PE largely unexplained, how do specific molecular PE polymorphisms map onto these liability-scale variance partitions?
