Spliceosomal disruption of the non-canonical BAF complex in cancer

One-line: Shows that the pan-cancer splicing factor mutation sf3b1 exerts a common oncogenic effect by mis-splicing a single gene — brd9 — via inclusion of an endogenous-retroviral-derived poison exon, destroying the non-canonical BAF chromatin-remodelling complex, and that correcting this one mis-splicing event with antisense oligonucleotides reverses tumorigenesis.

Key takeaways

  • sf3b1 is the most commonly mutated RNA splicing factor in cancer, but a unifying downstream effector had been missing; a CRISPR positive-enrichment screen for NMD-target genes whose knockout mimics mutant SF3B1 converged on BRD9, a core subunit of the non-canonical BAF (ncbaf) chromatin-remodelling complex.
  • Mutant SF3B1 recognizes an aberrant, deep intronic branchpoint within brd9 and induces inclusion of a poison exon that introduces a premature termination codon, degrading BRD9 mRNA through nonsense-mediated-decay — a convergent effect across diverse SF3B1-mutant cancers.
  • The poison exon is derived from a primate-specific endogenous retroviral element (absent in mice), an example of transposable-element-exonization being co-opted by aberrant splicing.
  • Loss of BRD9 collapses ncBAF specifically (not canonical BAF or PBAF), through the BRD9 DUF3512 domain that binds gltscr1/GLTSCR1L, and redistributes the complex away from CTCF-associated loci (splicing-and-chromatin).
  • BRD9 is a potent tumour suppressor in uveal melanoma; its splicing-mediated loss promotes melanomagenesis, metastasis and transformation of pancreatic/cutaneous cancer cells.
  • Correcting BRD9 mis-splicing with antisense-oligonucleotides or CRISPR-directed poison-exon mutagenesis restores full-length BRD9 and suppresses tumour growth in vitro, in xenografts and in a patient-derived rectal-melanoma xenograft — a mechanism-based splicing therapy rationale.

Notable claims / data

  • SF3B1 is recurrently mutated at specific residues in myeloid/lymphoid leukaemia and solid tumours: 14-29% of uveal melanoma (UVM) and 65-83% of myelodysplastic syndromes with ring sideroblasts.
  • CRISPR screen: 235 predicted NMD-target genes, 4 sgRNAs each, in Ba/F3 cells expressing SF3B1^K700E; BRD9 knockout conferred IL-3 (cytokine) independence and growth advantage; positive control was Pten loss.
  • BRD9 poison-exon inclusion was SF3B1-mutation-dependent across cell lines and patient samples (CLL, MDS, UVM) bearing 19 different SF3B1 mutations, but absent in healthy tissues. It triggered NMD, shortened BRD9 mRNA half-life and lowered full-length BRD9 protein; SF3B1-mutant patients showed reduced total BRD9 mRNA.
  • The aberrant branchpoint lies unusually close to the poison exon and lacks a polypyrimidine tract; neither U2AF1 (u2af1) nor U2AF2 knockdown affected its recognition, whereas inserting a poly(Y) tract forced inclusion even in wild-type cells. An exonic splicing enhancer was required (splicing-regulatory-elements).
  • SF3B1 mutation reduced BRD9 protein and abolished BRG1-GLTSCR1 interactions while leaving BRG1-BAF155 (canonical BAF) intact — selective ncBAF disruption. BRD9-sensitive genomic loci were strongly enriched for CTCF motifs (P < 1 × 10
  • HTRA1, a known melanoma tumour suppressor, was the most downregulated BRD9-bound gene under mutant SF3B1; correcting BRD9 mis-splicing restored HTRA1.
  • ASOs blocking the BRD9 poison exon prevented its inclusion, raised BRD9 protein and suppressed growth of SF3B1-mutant cells; intratumoural ASO delivery slowed growth of MEL202-derived and patient-derived SF3B1-mutant xenografts, but had no effect on SF3B1-wild-type tumours.

Connections

  • Establishes sf3b1 as the archetype of a cancer splicing factor whose oncogenicity runs through a single mis-spliced poison exon; complements the neurodevelopmental poison-exon logic in baralle-2017-alternative-splicing-as-a-regulator and general splicing-and-disease.
  • A companion example of a splicing factor acting as (or destroying) a tumour suppressor via a specific target, paralleling jbara-2023-rbfox2-modulates-a-metastatic-signature (RBFOX2/MPRIP in pancreatic cancer).
  • The therapeutic ASO strategy connects to splicing-therapeutics and the smn2/spliceai-era logic of exon-targeted oligonucleotides.
  • Links splicing to 3D genome organization via CTCF and ncbaf (splicing-and-chromatin, genome-architecture).

Open questions

  • How broadly does ncBAF/CTCF disruption explain SF3B1-mutant phenotypes across the many cancer types that carry these mutations, versus tissue-specific effectors?
  • Because the BRD9 poison exon is primate-specific, how should mouse models be engineered to faithfully test SF3B1-driven BRD9 loss in vivo?
  • Can systemic (non-intratumoural) ASO delivery achieve therapeutic BRD9 restoration, and does correcting BRD9 alone suffice given the hundreds of other SF3B1 mis-splicing events?
