Poison exon splicing in the human brain: a new paradigm for understanding and targeting neurological disorders

One-line: A genome-wide catalog of human poison exons (PEs) — alternative exons whose inclusion creates premature stop codons and triggers NMD — mapping their brain-specific and developmental splicing and 1,625 pathogenic variants that disrupt them, with CRISPR prime-editing validation in iPSC-derived neurons.

Key takeaways

  • Poison exons are conserved exons that, when included, introduce a premature termination codon (PTC) and trigger nonsense-mediated decay (NMD), down-tuning protein output. They are a specialized case of alternative-splicing coupled to NMD (the “AS-NMD” / poison-exon mechanism) and a recurring theme in splicing-and-disease.
  • A comparative analysis of 957 eukaryotic transcriptomes (Ensembl) shows Homo sapiens has the highest enrichment of NMD-targeted isoforms; 39.6% of human protein-coding genes give rise to >=1 predicted NMD isoform. The authors release the prediction pipeline as the tool evonmd.
  • Systematic in-silico annotation against Gencode v26 identified 9,814 putative human PEs (17.8% of 55,150 annotated AS events); 59.2% arise from cassette exons, the rest from alternative 3’/5’ splice-site usage.
  • Using rna-seq from GTEx and BrainSpan, they found 83 PEs uniquely detected in brain, 973 PEs differentially spliced in brain vs other tissues, and 1,284 PEs differentially spliced across brain development (before vs after birth). Brain-expressed genes are depleted (not enriched) for PEs, suggesting tight regulatory pressure.
  • Integrating ClinVar pathogenic variants with spliceai predictions flagged 1,625 pathogenic variants predicted to affect splicing of 743 PEs, enriched for seizures/epilepsy, intellectual disability, neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative disorders.
  • CRISPR prime editing in HEK293T and iPSC-derived i3N neurons functionally validated a subset; the deep-intronic NDUFAF6 c.420+784C>T (Leigh syndrome) variant increased PE inclusion, reduced NDUFAF6 protein, and impaired mitochondrial membrane potential.

Notable claims / data

  • Evolutionary scan: average 2.67% of coding genes per genome produce >=1 NMD isoform; vertebrates (3.7%) and plants (3.7%) highest, fungi (0.6%) and protists (0.1%) lowest. In humans, NMD isoforms = 20.1% of all protein-coding isoforms (14.3% under stringent TSL 1-3 filtering); 39.6% of genes have >=1 NMD isoform (30.3% under TSL 1-3). Robust to BUSCO completeness >=90% filtering — not an annotation-depth artifact.
  • PE definition (stringent, to minimize NMD escape): exon <=300 bp; not overlapping 5’/3’ UTR; on inclusion generates a PTC >150 bp downstream of TSS, >50 nt upstream of the last exon-exon junction, and outside the terminal exon.
  • CHX (cycloheximide) NMD-stabilization assay confirmed 87.5% (14/16) of putative PEs trigger NMD in HEK293T cells; same 87.5% rate confirmed in CHX-treated neurons. Non-responders (SMPD4, WDR45) reflect transcript-context dependence — downstream exon inclusion restores frame.
  • Disease ontology enrichment (UniProt KW): PE-containing genes enriched in epilepsy (137 genes), intellectual disability (313), neurodegeneration (181). KEGG: metabolic pathways (572 genes, FDR=0.00069), spliceosome (61), mRNA surveillance (43) — consistent with splicing factors autoregulating via PE inclusion (SRS/SF3 factors).
  • GTEx: 17,382 libraries, 54 tissues, 13 brain regions, 838 donors. BrainSpan: 607 libraries, 41 individuals, 16 brain regions, 10 pcw to 40 yr. PE inclusion correlated inversely with host-gene expression. Splicing factors RBFOX1, NOVA1, UPF3B enriched for brain-specific PEs (p=4e-6, hypergeometric). NOVA1 PE inclusion high in iPSCs, declines during neuronal maturation (validated by RT-PCR + CHX).
  • Variant pipeline: SpliceAI score cutoff |0.2| (positive = increased PE inclusion); 45,691 intronic vs 22,991 exonic candidate variants. Pangolin cross-validation showed strong concordance; SpliceAI higher sensitivity. ODiseA ontology mapping: 596 variants linked to seizures, 262 to epileptic syndromes.
  • Prime editing: 13 pegRNAs across 12 genes; 10/13 >5% efficiency, 9 >10% (avg 22.9%). Five representative validated variants — TAFAZZIN/TAZ c.284+110G>A (SpliceAI 0.57, +0.12 PSI), NDUFAF6 c.420+784C>T (0.23, +0.13 PSI), SLC12A6 c.2633-1G>A (0.99, +0.04 PSI), TSC2 c.4850-1G>A (0.99, +0.06 PSI), COQ9 c.521+1del (0.67, -0.28 PSI).
  • NDUFAF6 deep dive: c.420+784C>T retains 124 intronic nt, creating a PTC in exon 4. Clonal i3N neurons: WT PSI 0.13, heterozygous (C/T) 0.37, homozygous (T/T) 0.94. Homozygotes showed reduced NDUFAF6 protein (Western), impaired mitochondrial membrane potential (TMRM), and smaller/fragmented cell clusters; heterozygotes intermediate. Authors state this is the first functional characterization of NDUFAF6 deficiency from PE mis-splicing in a human neuronal model.

  • Therapeutic framing (NMD-sensitive PEs as targetable defects via RNA-based correction, e.g. ASOs) ties to splicing-therapeutics; the SCN1A “20N” PE / Dravet syndrome and SMN2 precedent sit in this lineage (smn2).

Open questions

  • How many of the 9,814 putative PEs are bona fide regulatory switches vs annotation artifacts of low-abundance isoforms (the authors caution annotation depth inflates human counts)?
  • Short-read PSI measures only overall exon inclusion, not transcript-context PE usage — long-read RNA-seq could resolve which isoforms actually carry the PTC.
  • Why does the heterozygous NDUFAF6 line show intermediate PE inclusion and MMP deficit but no significant protein reduction? What sets the dosage threshold for phenotype?
  • Can PE-targeting ASOs that block aberrant inclusion correct deep-intronic variants like NDUFAF6 c.420+784C>T in patient-derived neurons?
