Genetics of human brain development

One-line: A Nature Reviews Genetics review arguing that human brain development is governed by precise spatiotemporal genetic control whose human-specific features are now testable directly in human cells — by integrating cross-species comparative genomics, neurodevelopmental/neuropsychiatric disease genetics, and human pluripotent stem cell (hPS cell) 2D cultures and 3D brain organoids with genome editing to establish genotype–phenotype causality.

Key takeaways

  • Frames the genetics of human brain development through two complementary lenses — evolutionary specialization (human-enriched features) and brain disorders — and positions hPS cell-derived 2D cultures and 3D brain organoids, combined with genome editing, as the platform that converts the correlative evidence from GWAS/omics into causative genotype–phenotype links in the human system. This is the developmental-biology backbone of the brain-development-neurogenomics hub.
  • Lays out the conserved, stepwise cellular program of cortical development: neurulation → neuroepithelial cells (NECs) → radial glial cells (RGCs) producing excitatory/inhibitory neurons then glia, via intermediate progenitor cells (IPCs) in the SVZ, inside-out radial migration of excitatory neurons and tangential migration of interneurons, neuritogenesis/synaptogenesis, gliogenesis, and postnatal synaptic pruning and myelination.
  • Human-specific features are concentrated in neurogenesis and neuronal development, located in both protein-coding genes and non-coding regulatory regions such as human-accelerated regions (HARs); the dominant human signature is neoteny (protracted timing) — e.g., human RGC-mediated neurogenesis spans >3 months vs ~1 week in mouse and 1–2 months in macaque.
  • Outer radial glial cells (oRGCs) of the outer SVZ — a hallmark of gyrencephalic/primate cortex, virtually absent in rodents — are a recurring target of human-specific genes that drive cortical expansion and folding (e.g., ARHGAP11B, TBC1D3, TKTL1, TMEM14B, CROCCP2).
  • Disease genetics supplies the other axis: monogenic malformations (microcephaly, lissencephaly, macrocephaly, heterotopia) and neurodevelopmental/neuropsychiatric disorders (ASD, schizophrenia, epilepsy, intellectual disability, Rett, fragile X) map causal variants onto specific developmental steps, and many human-relevant phenotypes (e.g., ASPM, CDK5RAP2, DCX, FLNA, IER3IP1) are robust in hPS cell models but mild or absent in mouse — a central argument for human cell-based modeling.

Notable claims / data

  • Neoteny / timing: human RGC neurogenesis >3 months vs ~1 week (mouse) and 1–2 months (macaque); the primate subplate is ~5× thicker than other mammals’. These extended windows are argued to enable greater size/complexity/plasticity but also increased vulnerability.
  • Evolutionary neurogenesis genes (Table 1, modeled in animal and/or hPS cell systems): ZEB2 (delays NEC→RGC transition, faster cell cycle, greater cortical expansion vs apes; haploinsufficiency → Mowat–Wilson syndrome); HAR-regulated FZD8 (WNT receptor) and PPP1R17 (cell-cycle regulator) lengthen RGC cycle; NOTCH2NL (human-specific duplication enhancing Notch — deletion/duplication → microcephaly/macrocephaly at 1q21.1); ARHGAP11B, TBC1D3, TKTL1, TMEM14B, CROCCP2 expand oRGCs; ZNF558 (human-specific mitophagy regulator of SPATA18); DUF1220/NBPF dosage; PDGFD–PDGFRB signaling absent in mice.
  • Evolutionary neuron/circuit genes: FOXP2 (speech/vocalization, dendrite length, synaptic plasticity); SRGAP2C (human-specific duplication → neuron neoteny, increased spine density/connectivity); OSTN/osteocrin (re-purposed to brain, MEF2-dependent activity-induced dendritic growth in primates); CBLN2 (SOX5-regulated), PLXNA1 (FEZF2-regulated), EPHA7 (human-specific chromatin loop, elevated in subplate neurons); GATA3 upregulated in human maturing neurons to contribute to neoteny.
  • Cautions on evolutionary inference: TKTL1 and FOXP2 “human-specific” variant claims are contested — variants thought absent from modern humans appear when more ancestrally diverse genomes are examined; most studies are European-ancestry-biased; ectopic human-protein expression in other species can yield nonspecific artefacts; single physical-trait variants may not explain complex, likely polygenic human behaviors. Calls for resources like the more-inclusive human pangenome reference.
  • Disease → developmental step (Table 1, Fig. 5): microcephaly via centrosome/mitosis genes (ASPM, CDK5RAP2, CENPJ, WDR62), DNA-damage (MCPH1), ER secretion (IER3IP1); macrocephaly via PTEN/mTOR; tuberous sclerosis via TSC2; lissencephaly via LIS1, YWHAE, TUBA1A, DCX (X-linked, sex-biased severity); periventricular heterotopia via DCHS1/FAT4, FLNA; ASD risk epigenetic regulators ARID1B/CHD8/SUV420H1 (excess interneurons, premature deep-layer differentiation); “synaptopathies” — CACNA1C (Timothy), DGCR8 (DiGeorge/22q11.2), FMR1 (fragile X), MECP2 (Rett), SHANK3 (Phelan–McDermid), UBE3A (Angelman), DISC1/FGFR1 (schizophrenia); PLP1 (Pelizaeus–Merzbacher, myelination).
  • Environment: Zika virus-induced congenital microcephaly modeled in organoids/mice via TLR3, ANKLE2, adherens-junction and centrosome genes; severity depends on host genetic background; retinoic acid (RXRG/RARB/CYP26B1) gradients pattern prefrontal cortex and cortical–thalamic connectivity.
  • hPS cell limitations stated: iPS-line variability and need for line-specific protocols; organoids lack microglia/endothelial cells (mesodermal/haematopoietic lineages) and mature cytoarchitecture/circuitry; assembloids and multi-region patterning are emerging; high-throughput, reproducible organoid production is needed for genome-wide CRISPR/prime-editing and MPRA-scale (massively parallel reporter assay) variant screens; xenografting raises ethical considerations.
  • Genetic-architecture themes: genetic multiplicity (pleiotropy + polygenicity) revealed by single-cell multi-omics and GWAS for ASD/schizophrenia; cell-type-/stage-specific variant effects (e.g., SCN2A, CACNA1C, SHANK3, SYNGAP1 having distinct roles in immature vs mature neurons); CRISPR editing of schizophrenia risk variants (FURIN, SNAP91, TSNARE1, CLCN3) in isogenic iPS lines shows synergistic synaptic effects; DISC1 presented as a worked multidisciplinary pipeline (patient pedigree → isogenic iPS 2D/3D → druggable target → humanized-mouse rescue).

Connections

  • Anchors the brain-development-neurogenomics hub on the developmental-biology and evolutionary side, complementing the splicing/transcriptomic developing-brain sources.
  • Strong sibling to li-2018-integrative-functional-genomic-analysis (PsychENCODE/BrainSpan): both emphasize a developmentally regulated, spatiotemporal program and the convergence of neuropsychiatric GWAS risk onto specific developmental cell types/windows. Li 2018 supplies the multimodal molecular atlas and “late fetal transition” framing; Zhou reviews the functional/causal genetics and human-cell modeling that such atlases motivate. Both invoke shared risk genes (e.g., MEF2C, SOX5, TCF4) and the GWAS→developmental-context logic.
  • Complements walker-2019-genetic-control-of-expression-and (prenatal cortical eQTL/sQTL → TWAS): Walker maps the correlative genetic-regulatory layer (including splicing) that carries prenatal neuropsychiatric risk, whereas Zhou argues such candidate variants must then be tested in hPS cell models to establish causality. Shared disease focus (schizophrenia, ASD) and shared emphasis on stage-specific (prenatal) regulation; Zhou’s FGFR1 and DISC1 schizophrenia threads connect to Walker’s SCZ TWAS gene nominations.
  • The review repeatedly invokes massively parallel reporter assays (links to massively-parallel-reporter-assay) and non-coding regulatory/HAR variation interacting with neurodevelopmental genes — touching cis-regulatory-elements and 3D-genome rewiring relevant to chromatin-loops / genome-architecture (EPHA7 human-specific chromatin loop; HAR–gene interactions via altered 3D folding).
  • Disease-gene catalog overlaps the neuro wing of splicing-and-disease / splicing-and-neurodevelopment at the gene level (e.g., FMR1, MECP2, CACNA1C, SHANK3), though Zhou’s mechanisms are largely transcriptional/cytoskeletal/signaling rather than splice-variant-based.

Open questions

  • How many more human-specific regulatory mechanisms remain undiscovered — the review stresses only a small number of genes have been functionally validated, and that we have “only scratched the surface”?
  • Can organoid/assembloid platforms be matured enough to model later developmental stages, distinct cortical layers, functional columns, gyrification, and long-range circuitry — the steps where reliable hPS cell models are still lacking?
  • Will MPRA- and genome-wide CRISPR/prime-editing screens on high-throughput, reproducible organoids let the field functionally test “every GWAS risk variant” to establish causal genotype→phenotype links at scale?
  • How should ancestral diversity and patient-specific genetic backgrounds (currently European-biased) be incorporated so that human-specific and disease-risk claims (and polygenic scores) generalize across populations?
  • Which contested human-specific variants (TKTL1, FOXP2) survive scrutiny as more ancestrally diverse genomes (e.g., pangenome) are sequenced?
